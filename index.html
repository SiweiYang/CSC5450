CSC 5450: Randomness and Computation

This is an introductory graduate course on the use of randomness in computations and combinatorics. The aim is to provide a solid background for researchers to use probabilistic tools in computer science, engineering, or mathematics.

Lecture: Tuesday 2:30-6:15, ERB LT
Instructor: Lap Chi Lau
TA: Tsz Chiu Kwok

The course can be roughly divided into two parts. The first part is to introduce the basic probabilistic tools and probabilistic methods. The second part is to apply these techniques in various different settings. See last year's course hompeage for more details.

  • Techniques
    • linearity of expectations, moments and derivations (Markov and Chebyshev inequalities)
    • tail inequalities (Chernoff bounds), martingales (Azuma inequality), concentration of measures
    • random walk, convergence rate
    • algebraic techniques
    • probabilistic method, first moment, second moment
    • local lemma and its algorithmic aspects
  • Topics
    • Markov chain: Monte Carlo method, mixing, coupling, approximate counting.
    • Data structures: hashing, power of two choices.
    • Sublinear algorithms: data streaming, property testing.
    • Graph: shortest paths, minimum spanning trees, network reliability
    • Geometry: dimension reduction, metric embeddings, linear programming
    • Online algorithms: adversary models, paging, Steiner trees.
    • Derandomization: conditional probability, k-wise independence, pseudorandom generators
Notes: Course notes 2011.
Reference: Mitzenmacher and Upfal, Probability and Computing, Cambridge, 2005.
Reference: Motwani and Raghavan, Randomized Algorithms, Cambridge, 1995.
Reference: Alon and Spencer, The Probabilistic Method, Wiley, 2008.
